# Encore Editorial: Writing Style

This is the house style for everything published under Encore Editorial. It folds the humanizer skill (removing AI tells) together with our own voice. Apply it to every article, page, and byline before anything ships.

## Voice in one paragraph

Clear, sourced, and a little dry. We write the way Chris talks: plain sentences, the occasional deadpan aside, no hype. Think understated wit, not jokes. If a line would make a reader exhale through their nose, good. If it would make them roll their eyes, cut it.

## Hard rules (non-negotiable)

- No em dashes or en dashes, ever. Use a comma, a period, a colon, or parentheses. Scan the final draft for the characters before publishing.
- No emojis.
- Straight quotes, not curly.
- No chatbot residue: no "I hope this helps," "let me know," "great question," "want me to."
- No sign-posting: don't announce "let's dive in" or "here's what you need to know." Just say the thing.
- Every factual claim ties to a real, checkable source. Prefer .gov and primary sources. When the data is volatile, say so and link the source.

## Kill these AI tells

- Significance inflation ("marks a pivotal moment," "stands as a testament").
- Promotional filler ("vibrant," "nestled," "rich tapestry," "groundbreaking").
- Rule of three. Use the real number of items, not a forced trio.
- Synonym cycling. Repeat the clearest word instead of rotating through three.
- Copula avoidance. Write "is" and "has," not "serves as" and "boasts."
- Vague authority ("experts say," "studies show") without a named source.
- AI vocabulary: delve, underscore, landscape, intricate, crucial, pivotal, testament, leverage, robust, seamless.
- Inline-header bold lists ("**Performance:** performance improved"). Write prose, or a clean list without the bold labels.
- Generic upbeat endings ("the future looks bright"). End on a specific fact or a dry line.
- Persuasive tropes ("at its core," "the real question is") and fake-candid openers ("Honestly?", "Look,").

## Beat the detectors the honest way

AI detectors score predictability (perplexity) and rhythm variation (burstiness), not surface tells. Clean prose can still flag. So:

- Vary sentence length on purpose. Short. Then a longer one that wanders a little before it lands. Mix it.
- Keep one specific, hard-to-fake detail per piece: a real number, a named source, a concrete example.
- Allow a genuine aside or self-correction now and then. Perfectly even paragraphs read like a machine.
- Do not run our text through a paraphrasing "bypass" tool. It mangles facts and citations, which is the one thing this publication cannot afford.

## Voice calibration

When Chris (or any author) provides a writing sample, match its rhythm, word choice, and quirks before applying the rules above. The named author's voice wins over the generic default.

## Workflow

1. Draft the piece with the substance and sources right.
2. Run the humanizer pass against the patterns above.
3. Ask out loud: "what still reads as AI here?" Fix those lines.
4. Final scan for em/en dashes and emojis. Then publish.
