Birdeye alternatives, compared

Looking for a Birdeye alternative? Here's the honest field.

People search this for three reasons, usually in this order. The price: Birdeye's published range is $299 to $449 a month, per location, which turns into real money fast if you run more than one site. The contract: annual at minimum, with some buyers reporting three-year terms and cancellation that only takes effect on the renewal date. And the platform itself: Birdeye is built as a full reputation and marketing suite, with dashboards, social scheduling, and reporting, which is a lot of tool for a business that just wants more Google reviews. If any of that describes what you're running into, here are five real alternatives, what each costs as published, and who actually fits each one. (Full math on the Birdeye side is in our Birdeye pricing breakdown, if you want the specifics before you compare.)

WellEarnedReviews: done for you, single location

This is us, so take it as the pitch it is, but here's the actual difference. WellEarnedReviews isn't software you have to run. You text us a customer's first name and phone number after a job, and we handle the check-in, the review ask, the reminders, and the reply drafting. Five-star reviews get a thank-you posted automatically; anything rougher lands on your phone with a calm reply already written, waiting for your approval. First 30 days are $99, then $249 a month flat, or $174 a month billed annually. No contract, no per-location pricing, cancel with a text. It fits an owner-operator or a small team that wants the results of a reputation platform without becoming the person who runs one.

NiceJob: self-serve, budget-friendly

NiceJob is published at $75 to $174 a month, with a 14-day free trial and no credit card required to start, which is more generous up front than Birdeye's no-trial approach. It's a self-serve tool: you're the one logging in to send requests and check on things, so it fits a business that wants software to run rather than a service to hand the job off to. If you don't mind being the operator, NiceJob is the cheaper, lighter option in that category.

GatherUp: mid-range, with usage caps to watch

GatherUp is published at $99 a month plus $60 for each additional location, with a 14-day trial. The detail worth knowing before you sign up: plans are reported to cap out at 300 SMS and 3,000 emails per location per month. For most single-location businesses that ceiling won't matter, but a busier shop sending a high volume of requests should check the current cap against their actual customer count before committing.

Podium: still software, just a different price

Podium's published pricing runs $249 to $599 a month. It's a broader messaging and payments platform that happens to include review tools, not a review-focused product first, and like Birdeye, it's software you operate rather than a service that runs itself. Buyers report inconsistency around trial availability and complaints about annual billing terms. If Podium is specifically the tool you're evaluating against Birdeye, we've laid that comparison out separately in Podium vs. Birdeye, and if you're looking at alternatives to Podium itself rather than Birdeye, see our Podium alternatives page.

Starloop: flat rate, unlimited by design

Starloop publishes a flat $197 a month with no per-location pricing and no tiers, backed by a 14-day trial plus a 30-day money-back guarantee. Usage is explicitly unlimited, which is a straightforward answer to GatherUp's caps. Like the other self-serve tools here, you're still the one running it, so the tradeoff against WellEarnedReviews is the same one: lower price, but it's your task list.

Where WellEarnedReviews doesn't fit

We'd rather say this plainly than let you find out after signing up. WellEarnedReviews is built around a single location. If you run five sites and need one dashboard rolling all of them up, that's a real gap, and Birdeye or GatherUp's multi-location pricing is built for that job in a way we're not. We also don't do social media posting or scheduling, and there's no per-seat marketing suite or social scheduler. The tradeoff is the whole pitch: you do nothing, which also means you get nothing to configure. If what you actually want is a tool to run, not a task to hand off, one of the self-serve options above is the better fit.
