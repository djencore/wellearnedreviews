8 examples, one HIPAA rule

Dental review response examples

A dental practice can reply to a review without ever confirming the reviewer was a patient, and without repeating a single treatment detail they mentioned first. That distinction is the whole page.

Here's the rule. HIPAA protects patient information. A Google review is public. The moment a practice writes "we're sorry your root canal was painful," it has confirmed, in a place anyone can read, that the reviewer was a patient and named the treatment. The patient brought it up first, true, but confirming it back is the violation risk, not the original review. Every example below works around that the same way: answer the tone, not the content, and move anything specific to a phone call where records can actually be checked.

Praise that names the procedure, and a complaint about pain during one

REVIEW: "Dr. Patel made my root canal completely painless, I was shocked."Thank you for the kind words and for taking the time to write this. We're glad you had a comfortable visit.
REVIEW: "Cleaning was way more painful than it should've been, I was wincing the whole time."We're sorry to hear your visit wasn't comfortable. Patient comfort matters to us, and we'd welcome a call so we can look into it directly and privately.

Root canal never gets repeated back. Neither does cleaning, not as a word attached to this specific reviewer. Gratitude and a general apology do the same job without confirming who was in which chair.

Long wait time

REVIEW: "Waited an hour past my appointment time, ridiculous."We're sorry for the wait. That's not the experience we aim for. If you're willing to call our office, we'd like to understand what happened that day.

Billing complaint naming a treatment cost

REVIEW: "Charged me way more for my crown than the estimate said."Billing questions deserve a real answer, not a public one. Call our office and ask for the billing coordinator, she can pull up the details and walk through it with you.

Praise for a nervous patient

REVIEW: "I have serious dental anxiety and the whole team was so patient with me."Thank you for sharing that, it means a lot to hear the team helped put you at ease. We look forward to seeing you at your next visit.

The reply answers the feeling. Gratitude that the team was patient, without echoing "anxiety" as a word attached to a name, and without confirming a future appointment exists.

The misdiagnosis claim

REVIEW: "Told me I needed two fillings, went for a second opinion, turned out I needed zero."We take feedback like this seriously and would like the chance to discuss it directly. Please contact our office so we can review your care with you.

This is the hardest one on the page, and the reply does the least, on purpose. No confirmation. No dispute. Not in public. A diagnosis disagreement is a clinical question, and clinical questions belong in an exam room or on a phone line where records exist, not underneath a star rating where anyone can screenshot it.

Praise naming a staff member

REVIEW: "Hygienist Amy is the reason I actually look forward to the dentist now."Amy will be so happy to read this. Thank you for the kind words, we'll see you at your next visit.

Staff names aren't protected. Treatment details still are.

The dental review with nothing in it

Some reviews are just a rating. No comment, no complaint, nothing to confirm or deny.

REVIEW: [1 star, no comment]We'd like to understand what went wrong. Please call our office whenever it's convenient so we can look into it.
