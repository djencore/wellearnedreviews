Diagnostic walkthrough

Google reviews not showing up: why, and what to check

Short version: it's almost always one of two things. Either the review is still sitting in Google's processing window (usually 24 to 72 hours), or an automated filter caught it before it ever went public. You can diagnose both from where you're sitting, though only one of them is something you can actually fix. What follows goes roughly in order of how often each cause turns out to be the real culprit, so start at the top and stop as soon as one fits.

1. It hasn't finished processing yet

New reviews don't go live the second someone hits submit. Google runs each one through an automated check first, and that check typically eats anywhere from a few hours to three full days. Grab the timestamp the customer gave you (or whatever they screenshotted) and hold it up against right now. Under 72 hours? Stop here. There's nothing to click, nothing to escalate, and checking your listing every hour doesn't speed a thing along, no matter how tempting it is. Set a reminder for day four and go do something else.

2. It got caught by an automated policy filter

Every review runs through systems built to catch spam, fake engagement, and content that violates Google's review policies, before a human ever sees it. Here's the annoying part: a review can get filtered with nobody notified. Not you, not the reviewer. Once you're past the 72-hour mark, this is the most likely explanation on the whole list.

What trips the filter, in practice: a phone number, a URL, or an email address sitting inside the review text. Anything that reads like an ad for a competitor. Profanity or hate speech. And, worth flagging on its own, any review that mentions a discount or promotion tied to leaving it, which happens to be a policy problem on your end too (more on that further down).

Ask the customer to read the review back to you over the phone, or send a screenshot, and scan it for those triggers. Found one? There's no appeal button and no way to republish the exact same text; Google just doesn't offer that path. Ask if the customer is willing to post a clean version instead. That's the only real fix.

3. The reviewer's account looks new or inactive to Google

Google weighs a reviewer's account history right alongside the text of the review. No profile photo, no prior activity, no other reviews on file, and an automated system reads that profile a lot like a fake one, even when it belongs to a completely real customer who just doesn't spend time on Google Maps. Kind of a low bar, but that's the signal it's working from.

Have the customer search their own name on Google, or open Maps and check "Your contributions." Nothing shows up there either? The account itself is probably the issue, and there's no setting on your end that fixes someone else's account history. If it keeps happening with new customers, don't read too much into it: account history improves the more a person actually uses Google, and a filtered review this time doesn't doom the next one.

4. A cluster of reviews landed in a short window

Google's spam systems watch velocity. One review a month, then fifteen in three days, and that pattern reads as manufactured activity to an algorithm even when every single review in it is genuine. Ran a promotion recently? A review push, an event, a grand reopening? That's usually what did it.

Check your review count over the last two weeks for an obvious spike around when the missing review landed. Found one, that's a plausible cause. Nothing fixes it after the fact. Going forward, spread requests out instead of firing them all at once; this is the one cause here you can actually prevent just by how you ask.

5. Something changed on the business profile itself

Reviews can vanish, or look like they have, when the listing underneath them changes: an unverified profile, a recent merge with a duplicate, a business category swap. Google sometimes has to re-index reviews after a merge, and during that stretch they can disappear from public view even though nothing happened to the review itself. Log into Google Business Profile, or ask whoever manages it, and look for merge notices, verification requests, or category changes; a dropped total review count on the listing is a stronger signal than one missing review by itself. Finish any pending verification. If a merge happened recently, give it a few more days: reviews often resurface once the re-indexing finishes.

6. It was left on the wrong listing

More common than it sounds, especially with multiple locations or a listing duplicated under a slightly different name: the review is live, just not on the profile you're staring at. Search Maps for a second listing or an old closed duplicate, get it merged through Google Business Profile, and ask the customer to repost on the correct one.

7. It shows for the reviewer but not publicly yet

Sometimes a review is visible to the person who wrote it (right there under their own contributions) while the public listing hasn't caught up yet. That's index lag, not a filter, and it usually clears up within a day or two. Ask the reviewer to check their own account. Can they see it, and is it still inside the first week? Then just wait. Past a week with no change, treat it as filtered and go back to cause two.

What you can't do about a Google review not showing up

No form, no button, no paid service forces a filtered review back onto your listing. Google won't publish the exact rules the filter runs on, and it doesn't explain itself when something gets caught. Worked through everything above and it's still missing after a week? Google Business Profile support can take a look, but brace for a generic response and no timeline, not a specific answer. Be wary of anyone who claims they can recover a filtered review for a fee. Nobody outside Google can override that filter, full stop.

One more thing worth checking before you fire off your next batch of requests: sending a lot of asks at once is exactly what trips cause four.
