Field notes for single-property operators

Hotel review management

Seven a.m., the front desk, whoever opened that morning working through overnight email with the coffee still too hot to drink. Somewhere in that inbox, or it should be, sits a new Google review from a guest who checked out two days ago. Hotel review management really comes down to three things done on repeat: get recent stays onto Google, answer everything inside a couple of days, and catch it when three guests start describing the same problem before it hardens into the property's reputation instead of staying a fixable Tuesday. Skip one and the other two spend their time doing damage control for it.

Hotel review management starts with Google, not the OTAs

Booking.com, Expedia, TripAdvisor: real, and nobody's saying ignore them. But a guest reads those scores after already narrowing the search to your city, your price range, your dates. By the time someone's comparing your Booking.com number against four competitors, the shortlist is already built. Google works earlier. Someone types "hotels near [town]" and the map pack decides who even gets considered, before a single OTA tab is open.

There's a permanence issue too, and it's easy to miss. OTA review counts often reset or tie to some rolling window; the Google listing just keeps accumulating. A five-year-old property with three hundred Google reviews and a 4.6 reads as established. That same hotel, showing forty reviews on an OTA because the older ones aged off, reads thinner than it actually is. Google is where discovery happens, and it's the one surface that never resets on you.

Timing the ask

Send it after checkout. Not at check-in, obviously, and not three weeks out once the trip's blurred into every other trip that guest has taken. Right after departure, while the room and the breakfast and whatever the front desk said are still specific in someone's memory, is when a review comes out with actual detail instead of "nice stay, would recommend."

Text over email, and it isn't close. A guest gets a receipt, a loyalty nudge, maybe a survey from the OTA if they booked through one, all landing in the same inbox within a day of checkout. A review request in that pile is invisible. A text arrives alone and gets read inside minutes, assuming there's a mobile number on file from check-in or a pre-arrival form.

And send them one at a time, spaced across the days after checkout, not blasted to everyone who stayed that week at once. A property that usually earns a handful of reviews a month suddenly posting thirty in two days reads as manufactured activity to Google's spam detection, even when every review is a real guest telling the truth. Answering, and answering fast

A review posted overnight can wait until the seven a.m. shift opens it with their coffee. It cannot wait until next week. The guest who wrote it is checking to see if anyone read it, and so is everyone else deciding whether to book based on how the last complaint got handled. "Thank you for your feedback" on every single reply tells people nobody's actually reading these; naming the breakfast attendant, or the specific thing a guest mentioned, tells them someone is. Because someone is.

For the rough ones: thank them for the specifics, own whatever part was actually your fault, state the fix in one sentence, and take anything sensitive to a phone call instead of the review thread. Don't argue the star rating where strangers can read it. Don't negotiate a refund there either. Paste the review into the AI review response generator if you want a draft in that shape without writing it yourself, and negative review response examples has the worked versions for what actually happens at a property: late housekeeping, a noise complaint, a billing dispute.

What starts repeating

One review complaining about something is a guest having a day. Three reviews mentioning the same thing inside a month is the operation talking to you, and the category tells you where to look. Housekeeping complaints, room not ready, towels not restocked, some smell nobody can place, almost always trace to staffing or turnover timing rather than one person having a bad shift. Front desk mentions cluster around wait times at check-in or how a problem got handled in the moment, and they spike predictably around shift changes and the busiest weekends. Breakfast is its own category. It's nearly always running out of something by a specific hour, which is a supply and timing problem, not a menu problem.

Read these by category once a month instead of one at a time as they land. A single bad breakfast review is one guest's bad morning. Five of them citing the same nine a.m. shortage is an item for the ops meeting, not a coincidence.

Two rules, no exceptions

Never gate reviews. Never filter who gets asked based on how happy someone looked at checkout; send the same request to every guest. It's against Google's policies, and filtering also cuts you off from hearing the exact guests most likely to tell you something worth fixing. Never trade a discount, a free night, or a loyalty bump for a review either. Beyond the policy risk, an incentivized review stops being an honest opinion, and Google's own filters are built to notice language that suggests one was involved.

One more, specific to hotels: a public reply should never confirm that a named guest actually stayed, not if there's any privacy concern in the mix, extended-stay guests, corporate travelers on someone else's itinerary, anyone whose visit might be sensitive to acknowledge in public. Answer what was raised without repeating the guest's name next to dates or a room number. When in doubt, keep it general and move the specifics to a call.

Where this doesn't fit: forty flags across multiple brands with a corporate reputation team already in place. If that's your operation, you've likely already got tooling built for that scale, and this guide isn't really written for you. It's written for the operator running one property, or a handful, doing all of this by hand between everything else the front desk has going on.
